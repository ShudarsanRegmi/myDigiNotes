## :sd

### What is *parallel structure* (simple + intuitive)

Parallel structure means **keeping the same grammatical pattern** when you list or connect ideas.

Think of it as **pattern consistency**. If you start with one form, you should **maintain that same form** throughout.

---

## Core idea (lock this in your brain)

> Same role → same form

---

## 1. Basic examples (very clear)

### Correct (parallel)

* I like **reading, writing, and coding**.
  (all are *-ing verbs → consistent*)

### Wrong (not parallel)

* I like **reading, to write, and coding**.
  (mixed forms → messy, uneven)

---

## 2. With “to” (infinitives)

### Correct

* She wants **to learn, to grow, and to succeed**.

### Also correct (shortened but still parallel)

* She wants **to learn, grow, and succeed**.

### Wrong

* She wants **to learn, growing, and succeed**.

---

## 3. With conjunctions (and, or, but)

### Correct

* He is **smart and hardworking**.
  (adjective + adjective)

### Wrong

* He is **smart and works hard**.
  (adjective + verb phrase → mismatch)

✔ Fix:

* He is **smart and hardworking**
  or
* He **works hard and thinks smartly**

---

## 4. In comparisons (very important)

### Correct

* She likes **swimming more than running**.

### Wrong

* She likes **swimming more than to run**.

---

## 5. In long sentences (exam-level trap)

### Wrong

* The job requires **attention to detail, being organized, and to communicate clearly**.

### Correct

* The job requires **attention to detail, organization, and clear communication**
  or
* The job requires **being attentive, being organized, and communicating clearly**

---

## 6. Your earlier question (revisited)

* She decided **to leave her job and travelling the world** ❌
* She decided **to leave her job and travel the world** ✔

---

## Why parallelism matters (real insight)

* Makes sentences **smooth and rhythmic**
* Improves **clarity and readability**
* Avoids a **clumsy or awkward tone**
